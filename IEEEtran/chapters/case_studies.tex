\section{case studies}

In this chapter two case studies will be presented that highlight the effects of using biofeedback. The use different approaches. One uses the heart beat and the other uses EEG. They are both important for validating stress reduction and consciousness regulation.

\subsection{The "Unwind" Musical Biofeedback System}

The "Unwind" system, developed by Yu et al. (2018), employs a special approach to mapping the heart rate to music. It uses nature soundscapes combined with sedative music. Sedative music is used in music therapy to effectively decrease heart rate and blood pressure. It has a low tempo and a repetitive structure\cite{yu2018unwind}.

The system uses the heart rate variability to provide feedback on the user's breathing patterns. The measurement were done using a physical sensor. The heart rate variability is high during inhalation and low during exhalation. The framework maps these fluctuations directly to a wind soundscape. The wind volume is reduced during inhalation and during exhalation the wind becomes louder. The user should adapt his breathing pattern in a way that a the wind sound is like a smooth, consistent sinve wave. This promotes consistent respiration\cite{yu2018unwind}.

The wind provides immediate feedback on the breathing pattern. The overall relaxation is mapped with a special form of heart rate variability. The system calculates the variability with a sliding window of 16 heartbeats. The organic soundscape is based on the intensity of the heart rate variabilitys. If the variability is low, the environment becomes busy and crowded with bird calls and noises. On the other hand, if the variability increases, the soundscape becomes simpler and more transparent. The frequency of animal sounds decreases and the overall volume drops. This rewards the person with acoustic clarity\cite{yu2018unwind}.

The system was validated with 40 young adults. A significant decrease in heart rate was found in the music assisted conditions, and the reduction in respiration rate was significantly greater with biofeedback than without. In follow-up interviews, 70\% of participants reported that the nature sounds allowed them to quickly imagine natural scenes. This helped them to focus \cite{yu2018unwind}.

\subsection{Case Study: Mindfulness}
This study investigated if a system with biofeedback could lower the entry barriers of classical meditation. The goal of the experiment was to improve the emotional regulation and the cognitive performance of the participants. The experiment had a duration of weeks and 40 participant took part in the experiment who experience mild to moderate stress. They participants were split up into an experimental group and an active control group. As a tracking device, this experiment used the Muse™ headband (Figure \ref{fig:muse}). This is a mobile EEG system connected to a smartphone app. The participants had to do daily, active attention training sessions. The particiapnts were able to gather real-time feedback with the Muse™ headband. The recorded brainwaves were translated into acoustic feedback. The active control group performed identical relaxation exercises for the same duration but without the wearable device or acoustic feedback \cite{app7121280}.

The results after four weeks were significant and underscore the potential of wearables in professional stress management. The control group did not show large changes. On the other hand, the experimental group recorded a decrease in perceived stress of 7\% after two weeks. After weeks it went down to 16\%. In addition, participants demonstrated higher concentration levels and better impulse control during complex tasks. Moreover, the participants' brainwaves showed a lasting shift toward a relaxed mind. The central finding of Balconi et al. is that real-time feedback is the decisive factor for success \cite{app7121280}.
